Theft Bail Bonds for LGBTQ+ Defendants — California Charges + Discrimination Protections

LGBTQ+ defendants facing theft or burglary charges in California have the same legal protections and bail rights as any other defendant — plus specific safeguards covering jail housing, anti-discrimination, and hate-crime enhancements. This guide explains California theft statutes, the protections that apply specifically to LGBTQ+ defendants, and how to identify a bail bondsman who will treat your loved one fairly.

California Theft Charges Apply Equally

The bail amount for a theft charge is determined by the charge code and surrounding circumstances — never by the defendant's sexual orientation or gender identity. The most common California theft statutes:

  • PC §484/§490.2 petty theft (≤$950) — misdemeanor, $500-$5,000 bail
  • PC §487 grand theft (>$950) — wobbler, $20,000 misdemeanor / $50,000 felony
  • PC §459.5 shoplifting (≤$950 commercial entry) — misdemeanor, $500-$5,000
  • PC §459 first-degree burglary (residential) — felony, $50,000-$100,000, always a strike
  • PC §459 second-degree burglary (commercial) — wobbler, $20,000-$50,000
  • PC §211 robbery (theft + force/fear) — felony, $100,000-$250,000+, always a strike

The 10% bondsman premium under California Insurance Code §1800.4 applies the same as for any other defendant.

Bail Decisions Cannot Factor Identity (PC §1275)

Under California Penal Code §1275, judges set bail based on:

  • Seriousness of the offense
  • Prior criminal record
  • Likelihood of flight
  • Public safety concerns

Sexual orientation, gender identity, gender expression, and marital status are NOT permissible factors. The California Fair Employment and Housing Act and Government Code §11135 prohibit discrimination by state-funded entities (including courts) on these grounds.

If a defendant believes bail or release conditions were imposed based on LGBTQ+ identity, this is grounds for a motion under PC §1275.1 to challenge the bail amount, plus a complaint to the State Bar against any prosecutor or court personnel involved.

Jail Housing — SB 132 + Federal PREA

California's Transgender Respect, Agency, and Dignity Act (SB 132, signed 2020) requires California prisons and county jails to:

  1. Ask incarcerated transgender, non-binary, and intersex people their gender identity
  2. Use the person's correct name and pronouns
  3. House them according to their stated gender identity unless management or safety concerns require otherwise
  4. Document and review any housing decision contrary to the person's stated identity

The federal Prison Rape Elimination Act (PREA) requires all jails and prisons to screen incarcerated people for vulnerability to sexual abuse — LGBTQ+ status is a recognized risk factor. PREA-compliant facilities must offer separate housing options for at-risk individuals.

If a defendant faces housing concerns, ask the booking officer about PREA-compliant housing immediately and contact a defense attorney. Document any denial in writing.

Bail Bondsman Discrimination Is Illegal (Unruh Civil Rights Act)

California's Unruh Civil Rights Act (Civil Code §51) prohibits discrimination by business establishments — including bail bondsmen — based on:

  • Sexual orientation
  • Gender identity
  • Gender expression
  • Marital status
  • Race, color, religion, ancestry, national origin
  • Disability

A bondsman who refuses service, charges higher rates, or imposes harsher conditions based on LGBTQ+ identity violates the Unruh Act. Penalties include actual damages plus statutory damages of $4,000 per violation under Civil Code §52.

Report violations to: California Department of Insurance (regulates bail bondsmen — insurance.ca.gov), Department of Fair Employment and Housing (DFEH), and the California Attorney General's office.

Hate-Crime Enhancements (PC §422.55)

The California Hate Crimes statute (PC §422.55) allows sentencing enhancements when a crime is committed because of the victim's actual or perceived sexual orientation, gender, gender identity, or gender expression. Enhancements add 1-3 additional years to the underlying sentence under PC §422.7 or PC §422.75.

This statute is critical for LGBTQ+ victims of crimes (e.g., theft motivated by anti-LGBTQ+ bias). For LGBTQ+ defendants, the standard PC §1275 bail factors apply equally — no enhancements based on identity itself.

Frequently Asked Questions

What protections do LGBTQ+ defendants have in California jails?
Federal PREA + California SB 132 (2020) — jails must use stated gender identity for housing, name, pronouns. Document any denial in writing.
How much is bail for theft in California?
Per county schedule: petty theft $500-$5,000; grand theft $20K misd/$50K felony; first-degree burglary $50K-$100K; robbery $100K+. Identity does not factor.
Can courts factor sexual orientation in bail decisions?
No. PC §1275 limits factors to offense + record + flight risk + public safety. Identity-based bail is challengeable under PC §1275.1.
What if a bondsman discriminates against LGBTQ+ clients?
Illegal under Unruh Civil Rights Act (Civil Code §51). Report to CDI, DFEH, and CA AG. Penalties: actual damages + $4,000 statutory under Civil Code §52.
What about hate-crime enhancements?
PC §422.55 adds 1-3 years for crimes motivated by victim's identity. Affects victim cases primarily; defendants face standard bail factors regardless of identity.
